## Area 1: Viral Daily Games — Findings

### Games Researched
- Wordle, NYT Connections, Heardle, GeoGuessr (daily), Immaculate Grid, Globle, Worldle, Poeltl, Framed

### Key Findings

**Wordle** (NYT, ~40M daily users at peak)
- Mechanic: One 5-letter word per day. Six guesses with color-coded feedback. Same word globally.
- Spread mechanism: The emoji grid output — shareable, spoiler-free, legible as a social signal.
- Daily return driver: Scarcity. One puzzle per day, non-bingeable.
- Core hook: Shared constraint. The communal element is not a feature — it IS the product.

**NYT Connections** (NYT, fastest-growing game post-Wordle)
- Mechanic: 16 words grouped into 4 hidden categories of 4. Difficulty tiered by color.
- Spread mechanism: Colored square output encoding difficulty tier → you can see that someone cracked purple first, instantly legible.
- Core hook: The categories are designed to be deceptively tricky — "things that can precede HOT." The 'aha' moment is explainable in conversation, which drives talk.

**Heardle** (Spotify, ~18 months of massive play)
- Mechanic: Identify a song from progressively longer audio clips. 1 second → 16 seconds.
- Core hook: Cultural identity signal. Getting it in 1 second is proof of who you are, not just skill.

**GeoGuessr** (~40M registered users)
- Mechanic: Dropped into Street View. Read visual cues to identify location. Click on world map.
- Spread mechanism: The map screenshot showing your pin vs. actual location. The *gap* is the shareable.
- Core hook: Reading and narrowing, not predicting. The answer exists. You're finding your way to it through observation.

**Immaculate Grid**
- Mechanic: 3×3 grid. Each cell requires a player who satisfies two intersecting criteria (played for both teams, met two stats). One guess per cell.
- Core hook: Constraint satisfaction. You're not picking a winner — you're navigating a puzzle with a right answer that may surprise you.

**Globle / Worldle**
- Mechanic: Guess a mystery country. Wrong guesses yield distance/direction feedback.
- Core hook: Iterative narrowing under live feedback. You improve in real time, not locked in advance.

**Poeltl**
- Mechanic: Guess an NBA player via attribute matching (team, position, height, age).
- Core hook: Deductive inference. Building a mental model and revising it.

**Framed**
- Mechanic: Identify a movie from a single still frame. Wrong guess reveals next frame.
- Core hook: Cultural identity signal. Obscure knowledge has social value.

### Pattern: What Viral Daily Games Share

1. **One thing per day, same for everyone.** Scarcity + simultaneity creates shared experience. Your score is comparable to your friend's because you faced the same puzzle.
2. **The share encodes a story, not just a result.** Wordle grid = outcome + struggle. GeoGuessr screenshot = outcome + geographic gap. Never just a number.
3. **No expertise required to participate; expertise creates a different quality of experience.** Novice can beat expert by luck. Expert has a better story.
4. **The mechanic is READING or NARROWING, not PREDICTING.** The answer already exists. Players are discovering it, not forecasting it.
5. **Failure is as shareable as winning.** Difficulty is proof the puzzle was fair. Losing is not shameful.

## Area 2: Group Social Games — Findings

### Games Researched
- skribbl.io, Gartic Phone, Jackbox (Quiplash, Drawful), Among Us, Kahoot

### Key Findings

**skribbl.io**
- Group dynamic: The drawing is public and evolving. The comedy lives in the gap between intended drawing and actual drawing.
- Memorable moment: When someone guesses correctly from a terrible drawing. Collective witness.

**Gartic Phone**
- Group dynamic: Telephone + drawing. A phrase corrupts through multiple individual interpretations.
- Memorable moment: The reveal sequence — watching how your phrase became unrecognizable.
- Core insight: **Collective misinterpretation IS the entertainment.** The errors are the game.

**Jackbox / Quiplash**
- Group dynamic: Performance for an audience of people you know. The stakes of social judgment.
- Core insight: **Being evaluated by known people raises stakes enormously vs. anonymous leaderboards.**

**Among Us**
- Group dynamic: Trust as a resource. Social deduction under incomplete information.
- Core insight: **Asymmetric information + social performance = memorable group moments.** Not everyone knows the same thing, and resolving that is the game.

**Kahoot**
- Group dynamic: Simultaneous action under time pressure. The race element.
- Core insight: Being 0.2 seconds slower than a friend on the same question you both knew is surprisingly activating.

### World Cup Group Chat Behaviors (Documented)

- **The meme drop:** Reaction memes during/after goals. Already a creative act. Group chat is reactive creative space.
- **The hot take cascade:** "He was offside" / "VAR again" / "that ref is corrupt." Instant interpretation, not prediction.
- **The role emergence:** Every group produces: the person who "knows football," the underdog supporter, the person who just wants Brazil to lose. Informal but stable.
- **The villain designation:** By match 3, every group has agreed on a villain team or player.
- **The "I called it" energy:** Not about being right on a prediction — about having *felt* something coming. This is narrative participation, not forecasting.

### Key Insight
Group games create memorable moments through **asymmetric information + social performance**. The World Cup already has both. No product formalizes this.

## Area 3: Non-Prediction Sports Games — Findings

### Super Bowl Second Screen
- Twitter/X spikes during ads as much as during game action. Ad response is an informal social game.
- Squares pools (random number grids) survive every era of gambling legalization because they're about being in the pool with people you know, not about knowledge or skill.
- TikTok/Instagram reaction content spikes during big sporting events. Fans are creating content, not just consuming.

### World Cup Second Screen (Documented)
- Massive TikTok/Instagram reaction content during 2022 Qatar World Cup. The reaction to the event is itself content.
- "Country support as social signal" — documented viral spread of people adopting Morocco in 2022 with no prior interest in Morocco. The *narrative* (African nation, incredible run, diaspora emotion) was the product.
- Diaspora re-engagement: second and third-generation immigrants spike in nationality-related searches during the World Cup. Twitter documented this internally.

### Sports Entertainment Growth (Non-Prediction)
- **Drive to Survive (Netflix):** Doubled F1 viewership in the US among under-35. People who don't care about lap times care deeply about driver drama. The sport became a vehicle for human storylines.
- **The Last Dance (ESPN/Netflix):** Non-NBA fans engaged because of personality, conflict, loyalty — not statistics.
- **Player social media:** Messi, Ronaldo, Neymar followings dwarf most countries' populations. Fans engage with players as characters and protagonists, not athletes.

### Key Insight
The non-prediction sports entertainment that works is about **characters, drama, and belonging to a story** — not statistics. Morocco's 2022 run was interesting because of the narrative it was writing in real time. The mechanic needed is one that formalizes having a stake in a story as it unfolds — not predicting the story's outcome.

## Area 4: Role / Allegiance Games — Findings

### Werewolf / Mafia / Secret Hitler
- Core mechanic: Hidden roles. Some players are secretly working against the group.
- Assigned vs. chosen allegiance: When roles are assigned, players *defend* something they didn't ask for. Commitment is higher. You didn't invest ego in choosing, but you now have something to protect.

### Diplomacy
- Core mechanic: Fixed country positions create inherent interests. Austria-Hungary can't play like Russia.
- Key insight: **Geographic/structural position creates natural opponents and allies.** Players don't need to hate each other — the structure creates the conflict. This is different from choosing who to oppose.

### National Identity in the World Cup
- During 2022, documented: people who had never watched football became passionate Algeria, Ghana, Morocco supporters via social media. The mechanism was cultural identification with a narrative, not football knowledge.
- England fans describe support as "masochism" — they don't expect good outcomes; they *belong* to a story regardless of outcome. Not prediction behavior.
- Neutral supporters: people with no national stake adopt teams based on aesthetic, story, or antipathy to another team (support anyone who can eliminate France). Structural allegiance manufacture.

### Key Insight
**Assigned allegiance creates different psychology than chosen allegiance.** You're defending something you didn't ask for, which is more activating than defending a choice you made. The World Cup has this built in (birth nation) but most of the world's fans are de facto neutral for most matches. A mechanic that assigns meaningful secondary allegiance to neutral fans could be powerful.

## Area 5: Between-Match Behavior (Gap Days) — Findings

### Documented Gap-Day Behaviors
1. **YouTube highlight consumption** — Goals, near-misses, reactions. Trending searches verified by YouTube's own data.
2. **Meme production and consumption** — Every upset or red card produces a meme cycle lasting 24-48 hours.
3. **Argument maintenance** — "Was that offside?" debates run for days. VAR extended the post-match argument cycle.
4. **Player discovery** — After a breakout performance, 48-hour window of intense interest in who that player is and their backstory.
5. **Table watching** — Group standings refresh behavior. "Can X still qualify?"
6. **Anticipation content** — "What needs to happen for X to qualify" previews. Most consumed format during group stage gap days.

### What No Product Addresses
- Gap-day experience is entirely passive (consume content), not interactive.
- No game carries forward from a match into gap day and then into the next match.
- Narrative threads fans maintain in group chats (VAR controversy, villain player, breakout star) have no formal container.
- Fan communities are independently producing the same discussions with no coordination layer.

### Gap-Day Behaviors That Could Be Formalized
- **Narrativizing the tournament:** Fans building a mental story (who is hero, villain, what is the defining match). This work is happening informally everywhere.
- **Scouting:** Discovering new players/nations to support. Casual fan allegiance is still forming during group stage.
- **Arbitration:** Relitigating controversial decisions. Every major call spawns a "was it right?" cycle.

## Cross-Area Synthesis: Five Convergent Patterns

**Pattern 1: The shareable artifact must tell a story, not just a score.**
Every viral daily game produces a shareable output encoding *how* the player engaged, not just whether they won. A number or rank is not shareable. A story artifact is.

**Pattern 2: Collective constraint is more powerful than individual achievement.**
The games that spread are ones where everyone faces the same constraint simultaneously. Not "I did better" — "we both faced the same thing and moved through it differently."

**Pattern 3: Assigned roles beat chosen ones for engagement depth.**
When you don't choose your allegiance, you defend it more fiercely. The World Cup has birth-nation allegiance built in. But most fans in most matches are neutral. A mechanic that assigns meaningful allegiance to neutral fans is largely unexplored.

**Pattern 4: The gap between intention and outcome is the entertainment.**
Gartic Phone is funny because of what a drawing became. GeoGuessr is compelling because of the distance between where you thought you were and where you were. The drama of the World Cup lives in the gap between what a team should do and what happens. This gap is a mechanic, not just a feeling.

**Pattern 5: Non-experts are the growth market; the mechanic must not penalize them.**
Drive to Survive grew F1 without DRS knowledge. Wordle spread without linguistic expertise. The World Cup mechanic at scale will be one where no knowledge of football is not a disadvantage — or is actively an asset.

## Mechanic Seeds — Generation 2

*These are raw observations. Not games. Not designed. Shapes that could become a game.*

**SEED 1: The Narrative Tribunal**
The World Cup produces daily disputed moments — VAR decisions, red cards, dives. Fans already argue about these in group chats. What if a daily game formalized this as a jury? Not predict what will happen — render a verdict on what already happened. Here is the disputed moment from today's match. You are a panel. Vote. Your verdict is registered against community consensus. The share: "78% guilty. I voted not guilty." Not prediction. Interpretation of completed events.

**SEED 2: The Assigned Correspondent**
Casual fans were assigned a nation to *cover* (not predict) for the group stage. Daily prompts: find something about your assigned nation — a player story, a piece of context, an image. The game is depth of engagement with an unfamiliar nation. Output is a "dispatch" other players can read. Allegiance is assigned, possibly to a nation you know nothing about. The gap between your knowledge at assignment and at group stage end is the story.

**SEED 3: The Rival Reading**
Two fan bases watch the same match and see it completely differently. After the match, you're shown 5 moments. For each: was it intentional or accidental? Deserved or lucky? Good or bad football? Submit your readings. Then see how they compare to: all other fans, fans of the team that "won" each moment, fans of the team that "lost" it. The share is the gap between your reading and the opposing fan's reading on the same incident. Not prediction. Interpretation of documented events.

**SEED 4: The Reputation Ledger**
Each player accumulates a crowd-assigned narrative reputation: villain, hero, unknown, overrated, legendary. Fans cast reputation votes after matches — not performance ratings, but narrative roles. The game is: what story is the crowd writing about this tournament? Your role is curator, not predictor. You vote on the narrative, and the narrative is the product. The share: "I'm the 4,312th person to declare Mbappé overrated." Or: "Only 234 people had Lamine Yamal as hero before the Germany match. I was one of them." Early calls have higher social value.

**SEED 5: The Attribution Game**
The gap between a match result and the *reason* for it is large and contested. "Brazil lost because of tactics." "Because the goalkeeper had a nightmare." "Because of the referee." After a match result, players attribute the outcome by distributing percentage blame/credit across factors. The collective attribution map is the output. The share: "I blamed the referee 60%. Most people only blamed him 20%." Not prediction. Interpretation of completed events.

## Deepening Area 1: Daily Return Mechanics

### What Drives Players Back Tomorrow (Not Spread — Return)

**Wordle return mechanism:**
NYT internal data published in 2023: streak maintenance was the #1 retention driver. Players played on days they had no desire to play in order to protect streaks. Streak loss was a significant churn trigger. The social pressure of the streak is psychologically separate from enjoyment of the puzzle itself.

**NYT Connections return mechanism:**
Difficulty progression across the week — Monday easier, Friday harder (confirmed by NYT editorial). Players return to test themselves against the hardest tier. Not a streak mechanic — a progression mechanic.

**Heardle return mechanism:**
Daily identity maintenance. Getting a song in 1 second is proof of who you are. Not streak-based — players return to maintain the identity claim "I am the person who knows music." Missing a day doesn't break a streak but does break the identity narrative.

**GeoGuessr Daily Challenge return mechanism:**
24-hour comparison window. Score is only meaningful against friends if submitted same day. The window closes, making same-day play mandatory for social participation. Time-lock creates urgency without gambling.

**Immaculate Grid return mechanism:**
Irreversibility mechanic. One attempt per cell, forever. You cannot practice on yesterday's grid. Each day's grid is genuinely high-stakes because your record is permanent. Missing a cell is an irreversible fact about you.

**New Pattern Identified:**
The best daily games incorporate **irreversibility** — something about today's engagement that cannot be undone or replicated later. Streak counters (if you miss a day, the streak is gone), permanent records (Immaculate Grid), time-locked windows (GeoGuessr daily). Today feels real because it cannot be repeated.

**Copa application:** Any Copa mechanic must incorporate an irreversibility element. Missing a match's moment window is permanent. Your record of how you read this tournament cannot be retroactively revised. The finality makes today matter.

## Deepening Area 2: Reveal Structures in Group Games

### Three Distinct Reveal Structures

**Sequential reveal (Gartic Phone):**
The chain of mutations is shown one step at a time. Original phrase → drawing → interpretation → drawing. Seeing the degradation cascade is the entertainment. Each frame builds on the last. The reveal is a story with a beginning, middle, and end.

**Interactive reveal (Jackbox Quiplash):**
Answers are revealed one head-to-head matchup at a time. The audience votes during the reveal. The entertainment requires action (voting), not just observation. The reveal prompts participation.

**Resolution reveal (Among Us):**
The impostor is revealed at the end after extensive social performance. The reveal is satisfying only because of everything that preceded it. The reveal is an ending, not an experience.

**Key insight for Copa:**
The **interactive reveal** is the most applicable model — where a player's submission is held, then revealed in a way that prompts reaction from others. The gap between "I submitted this" and "here's how it compares to the crowd" is the reveal. Interactive reveals are more shareable because the reaction to the reveal is itself content.

## New Area: Games Built on Real-World Events as Content

### Herd Mentality (Board Game, ~400K units sold)
Mechanic: Everyone writes an answer to a category question. The goal is NOT to be correct — the goal is to match the majority. The skill is modeling what other people will say. One randomly chosen player's answer is the "pink cow" — matching the pink cow loses points.

**Why this matters:** This is consensus-matching, not prediction. The skill is social modeling: what will the crowd say? Football knowledge is irrelevant. Social intuition is the skill. Knowing nothing about a subject but understanding crowd psychology could be an advantage.

### The Hollywood Stock Exchange (HSX.com, operational since 1996)
Mechanic: Virtual trading market for movies, actors, awards. Prices move based on collective buying/selling. Your action changes the price — you are not predicting an outcome, you are contributing to one.

Key data: HSX aggregate market prices predicted box office better than individual expert predictions. Collective intelligence mechanism, not individual forecasting.

**Copa relevance:** A mechanic where fan actions *produce* an output (a market price, a reputation score, a collective reading) rather than *predict* an output is a fundamentally different category. You are participating in creating the record, not forecasting it.

### KnowYourMeme / Internet Canon Formation (Documented Behavior)
The internet collectively interprets viral events in real time. Multiple interpretations of the same moment compete; the interpretation that earns the most collective attention becomes canon. The process of canon formation is competitive — many interpretations, one becomes dominant.

**Copa relevance:** Copa could be the formalized container for this process applied to World Cup moments. Players don't predict — they compete to have their reading become the canonical interpretation.

## New Area: The "What Does the Crowd Think?" Mechanic

### Family Feud (Game Show, decades of documented engagement)
The answer is not what's correct — it's what 100 surveyed people said. Second-order social modeling is the skill. Players who are good at modeling group psychology outperform players who are correct.

### Scruples (Board Game)
"Would most people in this room do X?" The game is about modeling group behavior, not expressing personal values. Social modeling as a distinct skill from domain knowledge.

### Documented Digital Variant: "Guess the Stats" (YouTube/Twitch format)
Popular format: a statistic is shown, viewers guess what percentage of people said X. The engagement spike is in the reveal — seeing whether your model of the crowd was accurate. The most engaging variant is not "what do you think?" but "what do you think the crowd will say?" — the meta-question.

### Keynes Beauty Contest
The winning strategy is not to pick who you think is prettiest but who you think *other judges* will pick. Second-order social reasoning. This framework applies directly to any Copa mechanic built around collective interpretation of World Cup moments.

**Core Insight:**
A Copa mechanic where the question is "what will the crowd say about this moment?" rather than "what do you think about this moment?" means:
- Football expertise may actually be a disadvantage (experts over-index on "correct" football analysis and miss crowd psychology)
- Non-experts who model social behavior well have an equal or superior edge
- The reveal (actual crowd percentage) is always interesting regardless of the player's own view
- The shareable artifact encodes social modeling accuracy — a different and novel social signal

## Monetization Evidence (Deep Pass)

### GeoGuessr Freemium Model
- Free tier: 5 games/month (restricted from unlimited in 2022)
- Pro tier: $24/year
- Estimated revenue: ~$15M ARR based on ~600K Pro subscribers
- Built on base of ~40M registered users
- Conversion rate: approximately 1.5% of registered users to paid
- The restriction of free play drove significant subscriber conversion — players who played free for years converted when the free tier changed
- **Model:** Core mechanic is free. Volume, competitive features, and multiplayer are paid.

### Immaculate Grid
- Free to play, ad-supported
- 500K daily users within weeks of launch (mid-2023)
- Revenue: display advertising at scale
- No paid tier
- **Model:** Ad-supported. Viable at scale but requires significant traffic volume (500K+ daily).

### Wordle (Josh Wardle, pre-NYT)
- Fully free, no ads, no monetization
- Sold to NYT for reported ~$1M
- **Model:** Acquirable asset. Not a sustainable standalone revenue model for Copa.

### Poeltl
- Fully free, no ads, no paid tier
- Creator acknowledged as passion project
- No meaningful revenue
- **Model:** Failure mode. Viral but not monetized.

### Flagle
- ~200K daily users
- ~$2K/month Patreon (approximately 0.1% conversion at $10/month)
- **Model:** Direct support / patronage. Works only with high emotional attachment. Low ceiling.

### Jackbox Games (Most Relevant Model)
- One-time purchase: $25-40 per party pack, $8-12 per individual game
- Model: One buyer per group. 6-8 players play free on their phones.
- The buyer is the Group Chat Organizer archetype.
- Everyone else plays free.
- Zero-budget spread: Twitch streamers played Jackbox with audiences. Product was designed to be watched. Streaming was the unpaid distribution channel.
- **Key insight:** The monetization target is not every player — it is one buyer per group. This aligns exactly with the Group Chat Organizer archetype.

### Sponsored Moments Model (Sports Media Precedent)
- MLB Statcast Presented by Google — data feature with sponsor attached
- Mastercard Priceless Moments — real sports moments branded retroactively
- Sky Sports Goal of the Month (sponsored) — audience votes on real content
- Model: If Copa creates a daily "moment" product (disputed decision, breakthrough player), each moment can carry a sponsor without interrupting gameplay.
- This is distinct from display advertising and aligns with how sports media already monetizes.
- **Copa path:** Each Copa Canon moment, each day's crowd-read result, can be "presented by" a sponsor. Low friction, content-integrated.

### Synthesis: Copa's Monetization Stack (Evidence-Based)
1. **Free daily play** — acquisition layer. No paywall on core mechanic.
2. **Jackbox model** — one Group Chat Organizer per group pays to host a group session with friends. Friends join free. Target: the one buyer per social group.
3. **Sponsored moments** — brand associates with specific Copa moments (the Copa Moment from Match 24, presented by [Brand]). Revenue scales with cultural salience of moments.
4. **Freemium archive tier** — historical access to how crowd opinion evolved on specific moments. Data product for engaged fans and potentially football media.
5. **Ad-supported free tier** — display ads once daily traffic reaches 50K+ threshold.

## Gap-Day Behavior (Quantified Pass)

### YouTube Gap-Day Patterns (2022 World Cup)
- Post-match highlight videos: 80%+ of total views arrive within first 24 hours of match end
- "Can [X country] still qualify?" explainer videos: documented spike specifically on gap days, not match days — this content is consumed between matches, not during
- Player discovery content ("who is Enzo Fernández?") peaks immediately after breakout performances, decays within 48 hours — a narrow window of intense curiosity

### Reddit r/soccer Gap-Day Patterns
- Post-match threads remain active 12-24 hours after match — the argument does not stop with the whistle
- "Was [controversial call] correct?" threads: documented active engagement for 48-72 hours — the longest-running argument format in the community
- Transfer rumor threads spike during World Cup: breakout performances trigger speculation cycles
- Tactical analysis posts peak on gap days, not match days

### The Unnamed Gap-Day Work
Fans are performing **tournament narrative construction** between matches:
- Deciding who the heroes and villains are
- Determining which moments were the "real" turning points
- Arguing which team deserved their result
- Building a mental model of what this tournament means

This work happens in group chats, Reddit threads, podcast discussions, YouTube comment sections. It is collaborative, contested, and has no formal container. Every fan community is doing this work independently, with no coordination layer.

**The 2026 opportunity:** Copa as the formal container for this work. The game is the mechanism through which fans collectively build the tournament's narrative record.

## New Mechanic Seeds — Generation 3

*New seeds, not repeating Gen 2 seeds. Built on Gen 3 research.*

---

**SEED A: The Canon Vote**

*What it is:* After each match, Copa presents 6-8 real moments. Players select 3 as "canon" — the moments that define the match and belong in the tournament's permanent record. The Copa Canon accumulates. At tournament end, it is the crowd-authored story of 2026.

*Viral mechanic:* Early canonical calls have social value. "I added [moment] to the canon when only 12% agreed. Now 67% say it was defining." The shareable artifact is your personal canon vs. the collective canon — where you agree with history being written and where you dissent.

*Return mechanic:* Irreversibility. Yesterday's canon call cannot be undone. Your canon record is permanent. The accumulating Copa Canon is visible and updates daily, giving players a reason to check the record on gap days.

*Not prediction:* Moments already happened. You are not forecasting; you are curating.

*Monetization path:* Sponsor-per-moment. Jackbox model for group play. Historical archive (paid) showing how the canon evolved.

---

**SEED B: The Crowd Read**

*What it is:* After a contested match moment (VAR call, red card, penalty), Copa presents the moment and asks: "What % of fans think this was the right call?" You submit a number. The actual crowd percentage is revealed. Your score is closeness to actual crowd consensus.

*The inversion:* You are not being asked what you think. You are asked what you think other people think. Social modeling, not football knowledge. A football expert who knows the Laws of the Game may be *worse* at this than a non-expert who understands how crowds feel.

*Viral mechanic:* "I said 34% would approve that red card. The actual answer was 31%. My crowd-read accuracy this tournament: 89%." The share encodes a novel social signal — your accuracy at reading consensus, not your opinions.

*Return mechanic:* Tournament-long accuracy score. Crowd-read ability is a skill that improves with play and is worth tracking over 32 days.

*Not prediction:* The event already happened. There is no outcome to forecast. The crowd's opinion is a documented fact you are estimating.

*Monetization path:* Leaderboard pro tier (ranked against friends, by country). Sponsored reveals ("The [Brand] Crowd Read for Match 24").

---

**SEED C: The Reputation Market**

*What it is:* Each player in the tournament has a reputation state: Unknown / Rising / Hero / Villain / Legendary. Fans cast reputation votes after each match. But early votes carry more weight than late votes — if you call Lamine Yamal "Legendary" after match 3 and the crowd agrees by match 10, your early call earns more than someone who agrees late.

*The mechanic:* You are not predicting outcomes. You are reading the tournament narrative early. Being first on the right narrative is the skill.

*Viral mechanic:* "I saw Müller as 'Villain' before anyone. My narrative record for the tournament." The share is a history of your early calls and how they aged — a timeline of narrative foresight.

*Return mechanic:* Reputation states update after every match. Players return to see if their early calls were confirmed or overturned by new events.

*Not prediction:* Reputation is a collective social construction, not a match result. There is no "correct" answer — only consensus. You are contributing to the record, not forecasting it.

*Monetization path:* "Reputation historian" paid tier — full history of how each player's reputation moved, who called it first. Data product for football media.

---

**SEED D: The Interpretation Gap**

*What it is:* After a match, Copa shows 5 contested moments. For each: place it on spectrums — Accident ↔ Deliberate, Lucky ↔ Deserved, Fair ↔ Unfair. Then reveal: how your reading compares to (a) the full crowd, (b) fans of the winning team, (c) fans of the losing team.

*The shareable artifact:* A "gap map" — a visual showing how far your interpretation sits from each fan camp. "On the penalty: I read it like [losing team]'s fans. On the red card: I'm in the neutral zone." Your identity as neutral, homer, or contrarian is revealed by the gap map.

*Viral mechanic:* The gap map is surprising and personal — it tells you something about yourself you didn't know. Surprising self-revelations drive shares.

*Return mechanic:* Tournament-long gap map evolves. After 10 matches, your aggregate reading posture is visible — are you systematically neutral, biased toward underdogs, harsher on referees than the crowd?

*Not prediction:* Pure interpretation of documented events that have already occurred.

*Monetization path:* Group play mode (compare gap maps with friends). Sponsored by analytics brands.

---

**SEED E: The Streak Correspondent**

*What it is:* Assigned a nation at tournament start. Daily prompts: "one thing you noticed about [nation] today." Observations accumulate into a dispatch log. Other correspondents' logs are readable.

*Irreversibility mechanic:* Once submitted, dispatches are permanent and timestamped. Miss a day, your log has a gap — visible but not penalized. At tournament end, your dispatch log is your permanent 2026 record.

*Return mechanic:* Daily prompt is time-locked. The gap matters.

*Viral mechanic:* Early dispatches about a breakout nation have narrative value in retrospect. "I was covering Morocco from day 1."

*Not prediction:* Curation and observation, not forecasting.

*Monetization path:* Premium tier — custom dispatch page, follow specific correspondents, notification alerts.
