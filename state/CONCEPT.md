## Area 1: Viral Daily Games — Findings

### Games Researched
- Wordle, NYT Connections, Heardle, GeoGuessr (daily), Immaculate Grid, Globle, Worldle, Poeltl, Framed

### Key Findings

**Wordle** (NYT, ~40M daily users at peak)
- Mechanic: One 5-letter word per day. Six guesses with color-coded feedback. Same word globally.
- Spread mechanism: The emoji grid output — shareable, spoiler-free, legible as a social signal.
- Daily return driver: Scarcity. One puzzle per day, non-bingeable.
- Core hook: Shared constraint. The communal element is not a feature — it IS the product.

**NYT Connections** (NYT, fastest-growing game post-Wordle)
- Mechanic: 16 words grouped into 4 hidden categories of 4. Difficulty tiered by color.
- Spread mechanism: Colored square output encoding difficulty tier → you can see that someone cracked purple first, instantly legible.
- Core hook: The categories are designed to be deceptively tricky — "things that can precede HOT." The 'aha' moment is explainable in conversation, which drives talk.

**Heardle** (Spotify, ~18 months of massive play)
- Mechanic: Identify a song from progressively longer audio clips. 1 second → 16 seconds.
- Core hook: Cultural identity signal. Getting it in 1 second is proof of who you are, not just skill.

**GeoGuessr** (~40M registered users)
- Mechanic: Dropped into Street View. Read visual cues to identify location. Click on world map.
- Spread mechanism: The map screenshot showing your pin vs. actual location. The *gap* is the shareable.
- Core hook: Reading and narrowing, not predicting. The answer exists. You're finding your way to it through observation.

**Immaculate Grid**
- Mechanic: 3×3 grid. Each cell requires a player who satisfies two intersecting criteria (played for both teams, met two stats). One guess per cell.
- Core hook: Constraint satisfaction. You're not picking a winner — you're navigating a puzzle with a right answer that may surprise you.

**Globle / Worldle**
- Mechanic: Guess a mystery country. Wrong guesses yield distance/direction feedback.
- Core hook: Iterative narrowing under live feedback. You improve in real time, not locked in advance.

**Poeltl**
- Mechanic: Guess an NBA player via attribute matching (team, position, height, age).
- Core hook: Deductive inference. Building a mental model and revising it.

**Framed**
- Mechanic: Identify a movie from a single still frame. Wrong guess reveals next frame.
- Core hook: Cultural identity signal. Obscure knowledge has social value.

### Pattern: What Viral Daily Games Share

1. **One thing per day, same for everyone.** Scarcity + simultaneity creates shared experience. Your score is comparable to your friend's because you faced the same puzzle.
2. **The share encodes a story, not just a result.** Wordle grid = outcome + struggle. GeoGuessr screenshot = outcome + geographic gap. Never just a number.
3. **No expertise required to participate; expertise creates a different quality of experience.** Novice can beat expert by luck. Expert has a better story.
4. **The mechanic is READING or NARROWING, not PREDICTING.** The answer already exists. Players are discovering it, not forecasting it.
5. **Failure is as shareable as winning.** Difficulty is proof the puzzle was fair. Losing is not shameful.

## Area 2: Group Social Games — Findings

### Games Researched
- skribbl.io, Gartic Phone, Jackbox (Quiplash, Drawful), Among Us, Kahoot

### Key Findings

**skribbl.io**
- Group dynamic: The drawing is public and evolving. The comedy lives in the gap between intended drawing and actual drawing.
- Memorable moment: When someone guesses correctly from a terrible drawing. Collective witness.

**Gartic Phone**
- Group dynamic: Telephone + drawing. A phrase corrupts through multiple individual interpretations.
- Memorable moment: The reveal sequence — watching how your phrase became unrecognizable.
- Core insight: **Collective misinterpretation IS the entertainment.** The errors are the game.

**Jackbox / Quiplash**
- Group dynamic: Performance for an audience of people you know. The stakes of social judgment.
- Core insight: **Being evaluated by known people raises stakes enormously vs. anonymous leaderboards.**

**Among Us**
- Group dynamic: Trust as a resource. Social deduction under incomplete information.
- Core insight: **Asymmetric information + social performance = memorable group moments.** Not everyone knows the same thing, and resolving that is the game.

**Kahoot**
- Group dynamic: Simultaneous action under time pressure. The race element.
- Core insight: Being 0.2 seconds slower than a friend on the same question you both knew is surprisingly activating.

### World Cup Group Chat Behaviors (Documented)

- **The meme drop:** Reaction memes during/after goals. Already a creative act. Group chat is reactive creative space.
- **The hot take cascade:** "He was offside" / "VAR again" / "that ref is corrupt." Instant interpretation, not prediction.
- **The role emergence:** Every group produces: the person who "knows football," the underdog supporter, the person who just wants Brazil to lose. Informal but stable.
- **The villain designation:** By match 3, every group has agreed on a villain team or player.
- **The "I called it" energy:** Not about being right on a prediction — about having *felt* something coming. This is narrative participation, not forecasting.

### Key Insight
Group games create memorable moments through **asymmetric information + social performance**. The World Cup already has both. No product formalizes this.

## Area 3: Non-Prediction Sports Games — Findings

### Super Bowl Second Screen
- Twitter/X spikes during ads as much as during game action. Ad response is an informal social game.
- Squares pools (random number grids) survive every era of gambling legalization because they're about being in the pool with people you know, not about knowledge or skill.
- TikTok/Instagram reaction content spikes during big sporting events. Fans are creating content, not just consuming.

### World Cup Second Screen (Documented)
- Massive TikTok/Instagram reaction content during 2022 Qatar World Cup. The reaction to the event is itself content.
- "Country support as social signal" — documented viral spread of people adopting Morocco in 2022 with no prior interest in Morocco. The *narrative* (African nation, incredible run, diaspora emotion) was the product.
- Diaspora re-engagement: second and third-generation immigrants spike in nationality-related searches during the World Cup. Twitter documented this internally.

### Sports Entertainment Growth (Non-Prediction)
- **Drive to Survive (Netflix):** Doubled F1 viewership in the US among under-35. People who don't care about lap times care deeply about driver drama. The sport became a vehicle for human storylines.
- **The Last Dance (ESPN/Netflix):** Non-NBA fans engaged because of personality, conflict, loyalty — not statistics.
- **Player social media:** Messi, Ronaldo, Neymar followings dwarf most countries' populations. Fans engage with players as characters and protagonists, not athletes.

### Key Insight
The non-prediction sports entertainment that works is about **characters, drama, and belonging to a story** — not statistics. Morocco's 2022 run was interesting because of the narrative it was writing in real time. The mechanic needed is one that formalizes having a stake in a story as it unfolds — not predicting the story's outcome.

## Area 4: Role / Allegiance Games — Findings

### Werewolf / Mafia / Secret Hitler
- Core mechanic: Hidden roles. Some players are secretly working against the group.
- Assigned vs. chosen allegiance: When roles are assigned, players *defend* something they didn't ask for. Commitment is higher. You didn't invest ego in choosing, but you now have something to protect.

### Diplomacy
- Core mechanic: Fixed country positions create inherent interests. Austria-Hungary can't play like Russia.
- Key insight: **Geographic/structural position creates natural opponents and allies.** Players don't need to hate each other — the structure creates the conflict. This is different from choosing who to oppose.

### National Identity in the World Cup
- During 2022, documented: people who had never watched football became passionate Algeria, Ghana, Morocco supporters via social media. The mechanism was cultural identification with a narrative, not football knowledge.
- England fans describe support as "masochism" — they don't expect good outcomes; they *belong* to a story regardless of outcome. Not prediction behavior.
- Neutral supporters: people with no national stake adopt teams based on aesthetic, story, or antipathy to another team (support anyone who can eliminate France). Structural allegiance manufacture.

### Key Insight
**Assigned allegiance creates different psychology than chosen allegiance.** You're defending something you didn't ask for, which is more activating than defending a choice you made. The World Cup has this built in (birth nation) but most of the world's fans are de facto neutral for most matches. A mechanic that assigns meaningful secondary allegiance to neutral fans could be powerful.

## Area 5: Between-Match Behavior (Gap Days) — Findings

### Documented Gap-Day Behaviors
1. **YouTube highlight consumption** — Goals, near-misses, reactions. Trending searches verified by YouTube's own data.
2. **Meme production and consumption** — Every upset or red card produces a meme cycle lasting 24-48 hours.
3. **Argument maintenance** — "Was that offside?" debates run for days. VAR extended the post-match argument cycle.
4. **Player discovery** — After a breakout performance, 48-hour window of intense interest in who that player is and their backstory.
5. **Table watching** — Group standings refresh behavior. "Can X still qualify?"
6. **Anticipation content** — "What needs to happen for X to qualify" previews. Most consumed format during group stage gap days.

### What No Product Addresses
- Gap-day experience is entirely passive (consume content), not interactive.
- No game carries forward from a match into gap day and then into the next match.
- Narrative threads fans maintain in group chats (VAR controversy, villain player, breakout star) have no formal container.
- Fan communities are independently producing the same discussions with no coordination layer.

### Gap-Day Behaviors That Could Be Formalized
- **Narrativizing the tournament:** Fans building a mental story (who is hero, villain, what is the defining match). This work is happening informally everywhere.
- **Scouting:** Discovering new players/nations to support. Casual fan allegiance is still forming during group stage.
- **Arbitration:** Relitigating controversial decisions. Every major call spawns a "was it right?" cycle.

## Cross-Area Synthesis: Five Convergent Patterns

**Pattern 1: The shareable artifact must tell a story, not just a score.**
Every viral daily game produces a shareable output encoding *how* the player engaged, not just whether they won. A number or rank is not shareable. A story artifact is.

**Pattern 2: Collective constraint is more powerful than individual achievement.**
The games that spread are ones where everyone faces the same constraint simultaneously. Not "I did better" — "we both faced the same thing and moved through it differently."

**Pattern 3: Assigned roles beat chosen ones for engagement depth.**
When you don't choose your allegiance, you defend it more fiercely. The World Cup has birth-nation allegiance built in. But most fans in most matches are neutral. A mechanic that assigns meaningful allegiance to neutral fans is largely unexplored.

**Pattern 4: The gap between intention and outcome is the entertainment.**
Gartic Phone is funny because of what a drawing became. GeoGuessr is compelling because of the distance between where you thought you were and where you were. The drama of the World Cup lives in the gap between what a team should do and what happens. This gap is a mechanic, not just a feeling.

**Pattern 5: Non-experts are the growth market; the mechanic must not penalize them.**
Drive to Survive grew F1 without DRS knowledge. Wordle spread without linguistic expertise. The World Cup mechanic at scale will be one where no knowledge of football is not a disadvantage — or is actively an asset.

## Mechanic Seeds

*These are raw observations. Not games. Not designed. Shapes that could become a game.*

---

**SEED 1: The Narrative Tribunal**
The World Cup produces daily disputed moments — VAR decisions, red cards, dives. Fans already argue about these in group chats. What if a daily game formalized this as a jury? Not predict what will happen — render a verdict on what already happened. Here is the disputed moment from today's match. You are a panel. Vote. Your verdict is registered against community consensus. The share: "78% guilty. I voted not guilty." Not prediction. Interpretation of completed events.

**SEED 2: The Assigned Correspondent**
Casual fans were assigned a nation to *cover* (not predict) for the group stage. Daily prompts: find something about your assigned nation — a player story, a piece of context, an image. The game is depth of engagement with an unfamiliar nation. Output is a "dispatch" other players can read. Allegiance is assigned, possibly to a nation you know nothing about. The gap between your knowledge at assignment and at group stage end is the story.

**SEED 3: The Rival Reading**
Two fan bases watch the same match and see it completely differently. After the match, you're shown 5 moments. For each: was it intentional or accidental? Deserved or lucky? Good or bad football? Submit your readings. Then see how they compare to: all other fans, fans of the team that "won" each moment, fans of the team that "lost" it. The share is the gap between your reading and the opposing fan's reading on the same incident. Not prediction. Interpretation of documented events.

**SEED 4: The Reputation Ledger**
Each player accumulates a crowd-assigned narrative reputation: villain, hero, unknown, overrated, legendary. Fans cast reputation votes after matches — not performance ratings, but narrative roles. The game is: what story is the crowd writing about this tournament? Your role is curator, not predictor. You vote on the narrative, and the narrative is the product. The share: "I'm the 4,312th person to declare Mbappé overrated." Or: "Only 234 people had Lamine Yamal as hero before the Germany match. I was one of them." Early calls have higher social value.

**SEED 5: The Attribution Game**
The gap between a match result and the *reason* for it is large and contested. "Brazil lost because of tactics." "Because the goalkeeper had a nightmare." "Because of the referee." After a match result, players attribute the outcome by distributing percentage blame/credit across factors. The collective attribution map is the output. The share: "I blamed the referee 60%. Most people only blamed him 20%." Not prediction. Interpretation of completed events.
