# Learnings

## What Has Already Failed (Do Not Revisit)
- **Prediction Cards** (Gen 1-5, first run): Players pick match outcomes before kickoff and get a shareable card. Rejected — this is a prediction game, dozens exist.
- **Copa Calls / Live Instinct Calls** (Gen 1-10, second run): Binary yes/no calls during the match, shareable card after. Rejected — still prediction-adjacent. The timing is different but the mechanic is the same category.

Copa must not return to either of these concepts in any form, including variations or simplifications.

## Generation 2 — Research Cycle Learnings

### Viral Mechanics
- Every viral daily game's spread was driven by a shareable artifact that encodes a story, not just a result. Wordle's grid, GeoGuessr's map, Connections' color blocks. The artifact IS the distribution mechanism.
- The core mechanic of viral games is READING or NARROWING, not PREDICTING. The answer exists. The player is finding it through observation and revision.
- Scarcity (one puzzle per day) + simultaneity (everyone faces the same puzzle) = shared experience that makes comparison meaningful.
- Failure is equally shareable as winning when the mechanic is fair. This means a large portion of players who don't "win" still have a shareable story.

### Group Game Mechanics
- Group games create memorable moments through asymmetric information + social performance. Not everyone knows the same thing, and resolving the asymmetry under social pressure is the entertainment.
- The World Cup group chat already exhibits informal role emergence (the person who knows football, the person supporting the underdog, the villain-designator). No product formalizes these roles.
- Being evaluated by people you know raises stakes enormously vs. anonymous leaderboards.

### National Identity & Allegiance
- Assigned allegiance produces different psychology than chosen allegiance — you defend something you didn't ask for more fiercely than something you chose.
- The "neutral adopter" is the growth market: people who adopt a team based on narrative, not birth or history. Morocco 2022 is the documented case study.
- Most fans in most World Cup matches are de facto neutral. A mechanic that creates meaningful stakes for neutral fans would address the largest underserved segment.

### Non-Prediction Sports Engagement
- Drive to Survive (Netflix F1) is the clearest evidence that non-expert fans engage with sport through characters, drama, and belonging to a story — not statistics.
- World Cup second-screen behavior is primarily reactive and interpretive, not predictive. "That was offside." "That ref is corrupt." These are interpretation acts, not forecast acts.

### Gap-Day Behavior
- No product currently creates interactive engagement between World Cup matches.
- Gap-day fan behavior is: argument maintenance (relitigating decisions), player discovery, table watching, meme consumption.
- The narrative that fans are building informally (who is hero, who is villain, what is the story of this tournament) has no formal container.

### The Core Opportunity
The World Cup produces daily completed events full of contested interpretations. Fans are already interpreting, judging, and narrativizing these events in group chats. A game that formalizes the interpretation act — rendering verdicts on what happened, assigning narrative roles, reading disputed moments — would be:
1. Not a prediction game (events already happened)
2. Accessible to non-football fans (opinion requires no expertise)
3. Naturally shareable (your interpretation vs. the crowd's is a story)
4. World Cup specific (requires the volume of daily events, national allegiances, and real emotions that the World Cup uniquely generates)
